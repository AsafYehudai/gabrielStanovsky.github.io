PropS: Syntax Based Proposition Extraction

Semantic NLP applications often rely on dependency trees to recognize major elements of the proposition structure of sentences. Yet, while much semantic structure is indeed expressed by syntax, many phenomena are not easily read out of dependency trees, often leading to further ad-hoc heuristic post-processing or to information loss. To directly address the needs of semantic applications, we present PropS - an output representation designed to explicitly and uniformly express much of the proposition structure which is implied from syntax, and an associated tool for extracting it from dependency trees

Examples


  • Barack Obama, the young candidate, was elected president (details)
    • PropS
      • Two types of nodes: predicates (marked with blue background) and non-predicates (arguments or modifiers, marked with white background)
      • Each node consists of an arbitrary number of words (e.g., Barack Obama) and features (elect in past tense and passive voice).
      • Words in the graph don't have to come from the sentence (for example: SameAs, indicating apposition).
      • Not all words from the sentence participate in the graph - for example the, which is delegated as a definite feature on candidate.
      • Each predicate node heads its direct arguments, this can break the tree structure, e.g, the multiple heads for Barack Obama
    • OIE-like tuples
      • These are obtained by simple traversal from all the predicate nodes in the graph, omitting inner structure of their arguments.
  • She said that the boy is tall (details)
    • Dependency trees are great for verbal predications, as each verb directly heads its arguments. This doesn't hold for other types of predicates. We rearrange the tree structure so that this is true for other types of predications.
    • tall (adjectival modifier) directly heads boy and marked as a predicate.
  • If you build it, they will come (details)
    • For conditionals, we consider the marker word (e.g, because, although, unless, etc.) as a predicate, taking as argument both clauses, using the condition and outcome labels, as it introduces the logical relation between them.
    • Dependency, on the other hand, marks come as root, on which build is dependent, which, finding the conditional only as a daughter node of build, which requires the application to perform a multiple passes over the tree to recover the logical relation between the two clauses.
  • John, who is a nice man (details)
    • Different syntactic realizations may evoke the same proposition structure (e.g., passive versus active voice). We aim to present a unified proposition structure when applicable.
    • For example, note that this and the following example receive identical PropS structure, while the dependency trees diverge significantly
